## What AI Agents Can Actually Do in Legal Work

Let's be specific. We're not talking about some chatbot that writes terrible briefs. We're talking about specialized agents that:

### 1. Automated Contract Review
Reads contracts against your playbook. Flags:
- Missing clauses (you always include)
- Risky language (your firm never allows)
- Outdated terms (no longer used since 2023)

**Result:** 90% reduction in initial review time. You focus on the 10% that actually needs your judgment.

### 2. Due Diligence Automation
Pulls documents from data rooms. Extracts:
- Key clauses across 100+ contracts
- Red flags and inconsistencies
- Missing documentation

**Result:** What took 3 associates 2 weeks now takes 1 agent 4 hours. You still verify, but the heavy lifting is done.

### 3. Document Assembly
Takes your templates. Fills in:
- Client-specific details
- Jurisdiction-specific language
- Standard amendments

**Result:** First drafts are 95% complete instead of 30%. Your team focuses on customization, not boilerplate.

### 4. Regulatory Compliance Checks
Monitors regulations. Flags documents that:
- Don't meet current standards (GDPR, CCPA, etc.)
- Have outdated compliance language
- Need updates based on new case law

**Result:** You're always compliant without manually tracking every regulatory change.

## The Reality Check: What AI Agents CAN'T Do

Let's be honest. AI agents are tools, not replacements for legal judgment.

They can't:
- Give legal advice or establish attorney-client privilege
- Negotiate on your behalf
- Replace strategy and creative thinking
- Understand nuanced client relationships
- Handle novel legal issues without precedent

**What they do:** Remove 80% of repetitive work so you can spend more time on the 20% that actually requires a lawyer.

## How to Get Started (3-Step Implementation)

### Step 1: Define Your Playbook
Before AI agents can help, they need to know your rules:
- What clauses you always include
- What language you never allow
- Your standard review process
- Jurisdiction-specific requirements

This isn't "teaching the AI" — it's documenting what your firm already does. Most firms have this in practice guides. Formalize it.

### Step 2: Start with One Document Type
Don't automate everything at once. Pick the highest-volume, lowest-risk document:
- NDAs (high volume, low risk)
- Service agreements
- Employment contracts
- Vendor agreements

Build a workflow for that one type. Get it working perfectly. Then expand.

### Step 3: Implement "Human-in-the-Loop" Review
AI agents flag issues. You verify. This builds trust and catches edge cases.

**Workflow:**
1. AI agent reviews document (2 minutes)
2. Flags issues with explanations (1 page summary)
3. You review flagged items (15 minutes)
4. You approve revisions (5 minutes)

**Total:** 22 minutes vs. 60+ minutes for full manual review. **72% time savings.**

## The Ethics Question: Is This Malpractice Risk?

Valid concern. Here's how firms are managing risk:

### 1. Maintain Attorney Review
AI agents suggest. Attorneys decide. The human lawyer is still making the legal judgment calls.

### 2. Audit Trails
Every AI decision is logged:
- What was flagged
- Why it was flagged
- What rule triggered the flag

You can explain every recommendation to clients if needed.

### 3. Regular Validation
Firms test AI agents against past cases:
- Would this have caught known issues?
- Did it flag anything incorrectly?
- Adjust rules based on results

### 4. Client Transparency
Clients are informed that AI is used for initial review. Most don't care — they want lower bills and faster turnaround.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

### 1. "Automate Everything" Syndrome
**Wrong:** Try to build one AI that handles all legal work
**Right:** Start with NDAs. Expand once that works perfectly

### 2. Ignoring Edge Cases
**Wrong:** Assume AI handles every situation correctly
**Right:** Track what the AI gets wrong. Adjust rules. Iterate.

### 3. No Human Verification
**Wrong:** Let AI send documents directly to clients
**Right:** AI drafts. You review and approve. Always.

### 4. Forgetting Ethical Rules
**Wrong:** Use AI to give legal advice
**Right:** Use AI for document preparation and analysis. You provide legal counsel.
